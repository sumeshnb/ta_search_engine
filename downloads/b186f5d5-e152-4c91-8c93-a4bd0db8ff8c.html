Faculty Highlight- Assistant Professor Taylor Johnson

August 1, 2014
Personal website: http://www.taylortjohnson.com/
Lab website: http://verivital.uta.edu/

Computer systems are increasingly used for controlling critical systems like cars, airplanes, robotics, power electronics, and the electric grid. To ensure correct operation of these cyber-physical systems (CPS)--software systems interacting with the physical world--Dr. Johnson's research focuses on developing novel formal verification methods drawing on tools from software engineering, formal methods, control theory, and distributed systems.

Unfortunately, as anyone who has ever tried writing a little code knows, writing code that works correctly for some inputs is hard. Writing code that operates correctly under all possible inputs is even tougher. For computer systems that have the capability to control the physical environment, reliability is of paramount importance. There have been numerous near-disasters due to computer bugs in recent years, and even more recalls due to computer-related hazards by federal regulatory agencies like the National Highway Transportation Safety Agency (NHTSA), the Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC), and the Food and Drug Administration (FDA).

One technical problem that makes it so challenging to design these critical systems correctly is that the size of the systems' state-space and possible behaviors grows exponentially in the number of components. This problem is known as state-space explosion (or the "curse of dimensionality"), and Dr. Johnson's research has addressed this problem in fundamental ways for CPS. For example, one technique developed by Dr. Johnson and collaborators allows for reasoning about systems of any, arbitrarily large size using small, finite instances. Using this methodology, Dr. Johnson and colleagues developed a software tool called Passel that automatically proved that, in the Small Aircraft Transportation Systems (SATS) conceptual air-traffic control protocol developed by NASA as a part of the FAA's NextGen program, all aircraft were spaced sufficiently far apart to remain safe while landing. This approach received the overall Best Paper Award out of 155 submissions from the IFIP International Conference on Formal Techniques for Distributed Systems: Joint International Conference of 14th Formal Methods for Open Object-Based Distributed Systems and 32nd Formal Techniques for Networked and Distributed Systems (FORTE/FMOODS) in June 2012 at KTH in Stockholm, Sweden.

At UT-Arlington, Dr. Johnson has begun collaborations with systems biologists at UT-Southwestern to ensure validity of experimental data acquired in microorganism growth experiments. Through this, Dr. Johnson and his students have designed a novel continuous-culture device using the Beaglebone Black ARM development board and custom-designed printed circuit board (PCB) daughterboards, known as capes in the maker community. The software architecture uses the Xenomai real-time Linux extensions as a real-time operating system (RTOS) and Dropbox for online data storage so that remote monitoring of the weeks-to-months long experiments if feasible. A preliminary analysis of the design appeared at the 2014 Cyber-Physical Systems Week (CPSWeek) in Berlin.

Dr. Johnson became interested in computers as a child, playing early online text-based games known as MUDs (Multi-User Dungeons), and eventually going on to tinker with and modify the C source code for such games. As an undergraduate in Electrical and Computer Engineering at Rice University, Dr. Johnson developed an appreciation for safety-critical systems by working on both software systems and traditional control systems like motor control through several internships with Schlumberger. "At Schlumberger, I saw in practice the challenges that both software and controls engineers face in designing real-time control systems that have to work reliably, or the customer is losing potentially thousands or millions of dollars a day." In summer 2010 as a graduate intern, Dr. Johnson developed in conjunction with engineers at Schlumberger a novel approach for estimating the electrical power produced by a turbo-alternator used in downhole logging-while-drilling (LWD) tools, resulting in a patent application and the Best Paper Award from the 2012 IEEE Power and Energy Conference at Illinois.

The reliability challenges faced by Schlumberger are similar to those faced by engineers in other industries, where reliability and safety are paramount, like aerospace, automotive, robotics, and power systems. In addition to the NextGen SATS landing protocol verification, Dr. Johnson and collaborators addressed CPS correctness and reliability for aerospace systems through summer visits to Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL) sites. In summer 2011, as a visiting graduate researcher, Dr. Johnson developed an approach to automatically verify satellite orbital maneuvers at AFRL's Space Vehicles directorate at Kirtland Air Force Base in Albuquerque, New Mexico. In summer 2014, as a Visiting Faculty Research Program fellow at AFRL's Information Directorate in Rome, New York, Dr. Johnson developed an approach to automatically find cyber-physical specifications from embedded software source code.

"Being in academia gives me the opportunity to help address what the industrial and educational communities have come to believe is a serious problem in engineering CPS: we need interdisciplinary approaches to teach engineers skills from control theory, software engineering, reliability, and other fields, as the technical problems require a collaborative approach." From a technical side, such interdisciplinary approaches are also leading to solutions for the state-space explosion problem, for example by combining control theory tools with computer science tools.

Toward this interdisciplinary direction, Dr. Johnson leads the Verification and Validation for Intelligent and Trustworthy Autonomy Laboratory (VeriVITAL) at UT-Arlington. Other ongoing collaborative projects in VeriVITAL include:

  • design and analysis of distributed multilevel inverters for use in connecting renewables like photovoltaics with the smart grid (in conjunction with Prof. Ali Davoudi of Electrical Engineering),
  • indoor quadrotor localization using the Kinect sensor for use in control of guaranteed correct distributed robotic swarms (in conjunction with Prof. Amy LaViers of the University of Virginia's Systems and Information Engineering Department and Prof. Leonardo Bobadilla of Florida International University's School of Computing and Information Sciences), and
  • development of software engineering and hybrid systems formal verification tools and applications to CPS (in conjunction with Dr. Stanley Bak of AFRL).
